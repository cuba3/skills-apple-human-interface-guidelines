Writing
The words you choose for your app or game are just as important as the visuals. Thoughtful, consistent wording helps people understand your app or game and makes using it feel natural. Below are guidelines to help you write great interface text. For additional guidance, see Writing inclusively, and download a copy of the Apple Style Guide from the Books app or Apple Books Store.Writing style guidelinesBe concise. Get to the point quickly without sacrificing warmth. Use short sentences and common words. Avoid unnecessary words like "the," "a," "an," and "that." Instead of: "An alert that warns you about low battery conditions appears." Write: "Low battery warning appears."Use a friendly, approachable tone. Your writing should feel like a conversation with the user. Use contractions. Avoid jargon, technical acronyms, and formal language. Don't use "please" in interface copy, except perhaps in error messages when the user may have to wait or do something inconvenient. Instead of: "Please enter your password to proceed." Write: "Enter your password to continue."Use the active voice. Active voice is clearer and more direct than passive voice. It's especially important for error messages, where people need to understand what happened and how to resolve it. Instead of: "A password is required." Write: "Enter a password."Address the user directly. Using "you" and "your" feels more personal and is easier to understand than referring to the user in the third person. Instead of: "The user must enter a PIN to unlock the device." Write: "Enter a PIN to unlock your device."Avoid condescending language. Words like "simply," "just," "obviously," and "merely" can make people feel rushed or stupid when things don't work as expected. Instead of: "Simply tap Done to save your changes." Write: "Tap Done to save your changes."Be consistent. Use the same words for the same concepts throughout your app or game. Consistency helps people build a mental model of your app or game that they can apply in new situations. For example, if you use "New" to create a new document in one place, avoid using "Add" or "Create" for the same action elsewhere.Capitalize properly. Use sentence case for most UI text. Sentence case means capitalizing only the first word and any proper nouns. Use title case for window titles and other prominent headings. Avoid all caps for body text, except in rare cases where every letter of an abbreviation is capitalized (like "URL" or "ATM"). Avoid using all caps for emphasis; use bold instead.Localize carefully. Remember that your interface text may be translated into many languages, some of which can require up to 30 percent more space than English. Avoid long, unbroken strings of text that can be difficult to translate and hard to fit into UI. Also, avoid slang, colloquialisms, and culture-specific references that may not translate well. Avoid embedding text in images; place it in strings resources instead.Prepare for right-to-left languages. Some languages — like Arabic and Hebrew — are read from right to left. When you use system-provided UI elements, the system automatically flips the layout. When you create custom views, avoid using absolute positioning or mirroring. For guidance, see Right to left.Use appropriate punctuation. Use periods at the end of complete sentences in running text. Don't use periods at the end of UI elements like buttons, tabs, or short labels. Use ellipses to indicate that more information or another view is available, or that the system is busy. For example, use "Loading..." to indicate that the system is retrieving content.Use contractions thoughtfully. Contractions create a friendly, casual tone. Common contractions — like "don't," "can't," and "you're" — are generally fine. Avoid uncommon contractions like "should've" or "wouldn't've" that can be confusing. Avoid using contractions in error messages where clarity is critical.Writehelpfulerror messages. Error messages should explain what's wrong and, if possible, how to fix it. Be specific, but avoid blaming the user. Instead of: "Invalid email address." Write: "Enter an email address in the format: name@example.com."Avoid future tense. Instead of indicating that something will happen, describe what's happening now or what just happened. Instead of: "Your message will be sent." Write: "Sending..." When complete: "Message sent."Avoid gender-specific pronouns. Use "they" as a gender-neutral singular pronoun. Instead of: "When a user has a profile, he or she can edit it." Write: "When a user has a profile, they can edit it."Avoid potentially offensive language. Steer clear of profanity, slurs, and other potentially offensive language. Also avoid language that could be considered insensitive to people with disabilities. Instead of: "He's blind to the problem." Write: "He doesn't understand the problem."Avoid potentially harmful instructions. Don't provide instructions that could cause people to harm themselves or others.
