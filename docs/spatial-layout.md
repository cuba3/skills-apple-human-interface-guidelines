Spatial layout
People can make an indirect gesture without moving their hand into their field of view. In contrast, making a direct gesture requires people to touch the virtual object with their finger, which can be tiring, especially when the object is positioned at or above their line of sight. Include enough space around interactive components to make them easy for people to look at. When people look at an interactive element, visionOS displays a visual hover effect that helps them confirm the element is the one they want. Include enough space around the hover effect to ensure the element doesn't get obscured.Field of viewA person's field of view is the space they can see without moving their head. The dimensions of an individual's field of view while wearing Apple Vision Pro vary based on factors like the way people configure the Light Seal and the extent of their peripheral acuity.Center important content within the field of view. By default, visionOS launches an app directly in front of people, placing it within their field of view. In an immersive experience, you can help people keep their attention on important content by keeping it centered and not displaying distracting motion or bright, high-contrast objects in the periphery. Upright viewing  Angled viewing Avoid anchoring content to the wearer's head. Although you generally want your app to stay within the field of view, anchoring content so that it remains statically in front of someone can make them feel stuck, confined, and uncomfortable, especially if the content obscures a lot of passthrough and decreases the apparent stability of their surroundings. Instead, anchor content in people's space, giving them the freedom to look around naturally and view different objects in different locations.DepthPeople rely on visual cues like distance, occlusion, and shadow to perceive depth and make sense of their surroundings. On Apple Vision Pro, the system automatically uses visual effects like color temperature, reflections, and shadow to help people perceive the depth of virtual content. When people move a virtual object in space — or when they change their position relative to that object — the visual effects change the object's apparent depth, making the experience feel more lifelike.People can view your content from any angle. Including small amounts of depth throughout your interface can help it appear more natural, even in standard windows. Using SwiftUI adds visual effects to views in a 2D window, making them appear to have depth. For developer guidance, see Adding 3D content to your app.Use RealityKit to add extra depth when needed for 3D objects. A volume displays 3D content and functions like a window but without a visible frame. For guidance, see visionOS volumes.Provide visual cues that accurately convey the depth of your content. Missing or incorrect visual cues can make people uncomfortable.Use depth to show hierarchy. Depth makes an object stand out from other content, drawing attention. Changes in depth also catch the eye — for instance, when a sheet appears over a window, the window recedes along the z-axis, bringing the sheet forward and making it visually prominent.Avoid adding depth to text whenever possible. Hovering text over a background makes it harder to read, slowing users down and potentially causing visual fatigue.Apply depth purposefully. Use depth to clarify and enhance the design, not everywhere. When adding depth, think about the size and relative importance of elements. Depth works best for visually separating large, important UI elements like tab bars or toolbars from windows. It may not work well on small objects. Adding depth to small elements like buttons can reduce legibility. Also check how often different depths appear in your app. People need to refocus their eyes to see each depth change, and doing this too often or quickly can be tiring.ScalevisionOS uses two scale types to keep depth looking good while staying usable.Dynamic scale keeps content legible and interactive regardless of distance. Windows automatically scale up as they move away from the user and down as they get closer, appearing the same size at all distances.Fixed scale keeps an object the same size regardless of distance. Objects with fixed scale appear smaller when farther away, similar to real-world perspective.Use fixed scale when you want virtual objects to look like physical ones. Life-size product displays are a good example. Interactive content needs dynamic scaling to stay usable as it gets closer or farther away.Z-axis layeringConsider how objects layer along the z-axis. In visionOS, placing an object closer to someone makes it appear larger and feel more engaging, while distant objects appear smaller and less prominent.Arrange objects so important ones are closer to people. Position primary content and interactive elements closer than secondary content.Think about how object arrangement affects scene composition. Objects that are too close or too far can be hard to see. Think about how objects relate to each other when viewed from different angles.Placing objects near someone's line of sight can be more comfortable than placing them high or low. Try to keep most content near eye level.Layout windowsWindows in visionOS come in different shapes and sizes. A window displays 2D content, while a volume displays 3D content. People can freely resize and move windows, which is different from other Apple platforms.Window size influences how much content fits. Larger windows show more content and need appropriate spacing between elements to maintain readability. Smaller windows need focused, concise content.Windows have three size categories: small, medium, and large. Small windows work for quick interactions like settings or notifications. Medium windows work well for most app functionality. Large windows provide immersive experiences with rich content.Windows can optionally include a title bar. A title bar displays the window title and optional controls. Use it to provide context for window content. For guidance, see Title bars.People can place windows anywhere in their space. When placing content, consider where people might naturally look. Centered or slightly below eye level typically works best.PositioningAvoid placing content in areas that are hard to reach. People generally find it easier to interact with content at or below eye level. Content placed too high or too low can cause neck strain from looking up or down for extended periods. Placing interactive elements too far from someone can also make them uncomfortable to reach. Use proximity to communicate interactivity. When you place a 3D object far from people, they typically won't try to touch or move it. When you place a 3D object close to people, they're more likely to interact with it. This natural behavior can guide how you design interactive elements in your app.Place interactive objects where people expect to find them. Standard system elements appear in predictable locations, so placing app content nearby makes it easier to discover. Placing objects too close together makes them hard to select accurately. Give each interactive element enough breathing room for comfortable interaction. People use visual hover effects to confirm which element they'll interact with, so overlapping hover effects can cause confusion.Leave enough space around interactive elements for hover effects to display clearly. For guidance on minimum spacing between interactive elements, see Buttons visionOS.Hover effectsPeople look at interactive elements to select them, and visionOS displays a visual hover effect when someone looks at something interactive. A hover effect can include changes like highlighting, scaling, or other visual feedback that confirms the element under focus. Design hover effects to be subtle yet noticeable. Too much visual change can distract, while too little can leave people uncertain about what they'll interact with. When designing hover effects, consider how they appear from different angles, especially when someone views the interface from the side. Don't obstruct content with hover effects. Hover effects shouldn't cover important information or interfere with understanding the content below.Keep hover effects consistent across your app. Using the same hover effect styles throughout creates a coherent experience and helps people develop predictable expectations.Positioning content relative to windowsConsider where to place content relative to the window. People typically look at window content first, then at surrounding elements. Position the most important content at the top of a window or in the upper portion for quick discovery. Position less critical information and controls towards the bottom or edges of a window, following a natural top-to-bottom hierarchy that matches reading order. When there's secondary content like settings or options, place it below or beside primary content, but distinguish it visually so it doesn't compete for attention. When deciding between horizontal and vertical layouts, think about content width. Wide content works better in horizontal arrangements, while tall content works better vertically.Safe zonesAvoid placing important content near window edges. Window resizing can crop or hide content at the edges. Content near the center remains visible and accessible regardless of window size or position.Limit interactive elements near edges. Edges are harder to interact with precisely, especially with direct touch.
